For the last 4 years, Gustaf Josefsson Tadaa has been the industry leader for Exploring Technology in the Stockholm Digital Media Creative program. It’s a 5 week project where the students are tasked to create an interactive art exhibition. This fall, over 1500 people attended the latest iteration, artistically exploring the theme “Mother”.

When not at Hyper Island, Gustaf works as a futurologist and speaker, and is now nominated in the “breakthrough” category at the Speaker of the Year awards on Feb 6.

What do you talk about?

I tell stories about how technology drives change in society, how the accelerating technological evolution affects how we build organisations and relate to each other as humans. I then try to create these stories to learnings about organizational development, self-management and personal transformation.

Technology is what separates us from other animals, it is what have made us what we are. It is technology that constantly drives us to become more human. I think many people today are missing a narrative that enables them to relate to the change they see in society on a deeper level, which makes the whole thing kind of scary. Because of this I think the role of the storyteller is more important than ever.

What are your thoughts on education?

I think Hyper really nails much of where education is heading, with a focus on personal and interpersonal development, rather than specific kowledge.

The way I structure most of my classes at Hyper Island is that I give the students a piece of technology and ask hem to figure it out together. I throw them in to the deep and and the’ll just have to figure out how to swim. There tons of easily accesible resources out there, so why are we even in a class room? We can’t see schools as information distribution systems anymore.

My job can not be to deliver knowledge, but to invite the students to their own journey. I want to ignite the internal engine of curiosity, experimentation and pursuit of knowledge. I want to make my role as a teacher redundant.

You obviously spend a lot of time on stage. Do you have any advice for those who want to become better speakers?

Well, the first thing is to just do it. And then do it again. Looking back at how I was on stage a few years ago, I both laugh and become a bit uncomfortable. Was I really that bad? It’s not until the last 3 years, after having done hundreds of talks that I really have gotten good at it.

One way of getting out there more is to get in touch with organisations that book professionals to give talks in schools, to give students more stories from “the real world”. Transfer (transfer.nu) is a non-profit organisation that does that in Sweden. It’s a great way to get some practice, with a pretty demanding audience. I did a lot of talks through them as I was starting up and I still do several a year.

How about on stage?

Be as much yourself as you possibly can. My experience is that the more I dare to express what I really think an feel, the more respect I earn. There are too many audience-pleasing, bland, white-bread speakers out there, so if you don’t have the courage to share your authentic self, but rather hide behind buzzwords and cliché references to TED-talks, you might as well stay at home.

Dare to say the thing that you are not “allowed” to say. Dare to share the story that you are afraid of other judging you for. Dare to make people uncomfortable, angry, afraid or sad. If we mask out all the bad stuff, we will unable fully relate to joy, hope, passion and curiosity. Bring your audience on a journey. Practice empathy, listen in, be nice, but don’t make compromises on who you really are.
