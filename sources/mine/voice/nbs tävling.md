I’m here to make the argument that the only way for organizations to handle the complexity of the digital world is through the psychological development of every single employee.

I also want to make the argument that personal development can no longer be seen as separate from the digital world.

To be able to make this argument, I have to start by showing you the most overused graph of the last decade. I’ve been told that to win this competition, I’m supposed to exhibit ”originality” and ”freshness”… so I guess I’m already out. Cause you’ve all seen this slide before… it looks like this.

The exponential curve, or as I call it, the ”it’s going faster and faster nowadays”-curve. The cliché that is mentioned at both breakfast tables and board rooms. ”The world is changing fast, nowadays. Oh, yeah, faster and faster”. And that’s true… but it’s also nothing new. No matter if you look at the history if the earth, the evolution of life, the 12000 years of civilization, the last 2000 years compared to the last 200. No matter the perspective, it looks like everything happened in the end, as if the universe itself had a gigantic built in ”ketchup effect”, you know ”nothing, nothing, nothing, splortch”.

This is not new, it’s not something that a bunch of technologists in Silicon Valley came up with, its not something we can just stop or step away from. This acceleration of complexity seems built in to the fabric of the universe itself.

And this is the defining characteristic of our current age. The transformation age… in which we spend more time changing what we do than we actually spend doing it.

This is the organizational chart of Napoleon.

Napoleon was the first military leader who utilized the power of the printing press… he taught his soldiers to read and write!

This meant two things.

First, he could write job descriptions. He could in clear writing tell people what to do and how to act. This makes people more predictable and, of course, more replaceable, which is great in the army.

Second, he could draw lines between these roles, and design how information should flow within the organization.

So every soldier as they take in information, will know if they should act locally, or report.

Next person in the pyramid have clear instructions of how to process the information, to act here, or there, or pass it upwards.

Effectively transforming 600 000 people, spread over an entire continent, into one giant computer, able to process vast amounts of data in a predictable way.

That enabled, for the first time, for strategic decisions based on data!

This is brilliant! I mean, this is how we built the modern world: all our large corporations, or state, military, academia.

All because of the printing press, enabling the written language every single member of the organization

But then… along came the telephone, and someone would pick up the phone and call from here to here… that was NOT popular.

And today, this guy here will just ask a question on a hashtag and someone in a another organization will answer.

So, no company looks like this anymore. In the world, today, period.

And sure, she might still be the boss of this guy, but this is an influential partner, or client who has way more power than someone on salary over here. All these guys over here are consultants, these play tennis and these are sleeping with each other.

And it ALL THIS COUNTS. In how information flows and how decisions are made.

Using this data, they know you better than your own family, better than your own partner.

So why are we talking about it? Well, that’s because this is the first time we can actually feel the curve during one human lifetime. So what exactly is it that is changing?

Let me just hammer this in, using a term from the US Army, that some of you might have heard already. This termed was coined in 1987 and describes what, in this explosive change, is it exactly that we are getting more of. This term is VUCA

Vuca stans for

Volatility, we see that small changes in conditions can have a larger effect in a shorter time, because of the interconnectedness of our world

Uncertainty, in every part of our organization, in every project and process, we have to deal with more unknown variables.

Complexity, we also realize that these variables can not be analyzed by mere causality where a leads to b leads to c, but in every thing we work with, we have to deal with many independent variables that interact with each other in a complex way.

Ambiguity, it’s just harder nowadays to tell right from wrong, success from failure, black from white. More often we end up in that grey gooey slush in the middle.

This is not just the US army, this is every industry, it is every aspect of our lives. From how we relate to customers, to how we interact with our kids.

To make a concrete example of this:

If I, 30 years ago, said: ”I eat healthy”

You would pretty much have know what I meant.

I mean, there was the food circle or pyramid, and you were supposed to eat your fruit and veg. That’s it.

In March of 2019, if I say ”I eat healthy”… what do I even mean?

It’s volatile, it changes every week what it is to eat healthy now.

It’s uncertain, the more we learn about diet, the more we realize how little we know

It’s complex, we know now that it’s not just that something is healthy, but rather that there are complex interaction between your genes, your behaviors and what you eat. So that something that is healthy for me might not be healthy for you.

And finally, it’s ambigous, it’s harder now to tell healthy from unhealthy, right, from wrong. Most things end up in that grey scale in the middle.

This is an old study looking at employee commitment, in relation to how clearly they understand their organizational values.

The baseline here, on a 7 point scale, is 4.9.

But when they worked with employees to create clarity around the organizational values… that number goes to… 4.87.

In this context within some margin of error.

However, if you instead measure how clearly the employees knew their own personal values, and worked with those, the number went up to 6.12

Now the best scenario is here, when you both know personal and organizational values, but as you can see, you HAVE to start with personal.
