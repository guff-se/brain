**Love mushroom and temple at creative event.**

**This weekend, The Bizarre Star Bazaar happens at Nobelberget. A participatory festival where creativity has no limit.**

It feels like having fallen down the same hole as Alice in Wonderland when DN’s team go through the doors of Urban Burn - The Bizarre Star Bazaar, that happens this weekend at Nobelberget in Sickla.

The inspiration for the event comes from the american event Burning Man, that happens yearly in Black Rock Desert in Nevada, explains Gustaf Josefsson, one of the initiators.

There is no dessert here, but a 2000 square meter ware house where a fabulous and magical world is taking shape, completely created by the participants themselves. On the question if the event is for everyone, Gustaf Josefsson answers that he is constantly surprised for how many different kinds of people it can be for.

- I don’t know who everyone is or where to draw the lines, but it seems to be for more kinds of people than you could ever expect.

In total 730 tickets have been sold, and the participants comes from the Nordics and from countries like USA, Germany and Czech Republic. The age spans covers everything from babies, kids, adults and elders. Since the festival builds on participation, we immediately understand that this will not be a normal interview. All we can do is to roll up our sleeves and start participating.

In one of the corners, a gigant mushroom forest have started to take place. Everything from a giant Avatar tree, decorated with fibre optics, to giant mushrooms made from chicken wire, fibre cloth and Saran wrap fits in to the virtual forest.

Under a giant love mushroom stands Oden Lobell, Christopher Kummelstedt and Erik Haversten, covering the proximity sensors that have been placed on the mushroom.

- The love mushroom shines with the power of the closeness that exists around it. The more people who get cozy below it, the more life energy it gets and therefore the more light, Christopher Kummelstedt explains.

Almost every centimeter of the premises is covered with ongoing projects. A monkey mountain inspired by the “Jungle Book”, a mobile monster café on a tricycle, and a 25 meter long piano that sounds authentically when you walk on it, are just a few of the things that are going on. Gustaf Josefsson explains that the festival, which is the first of its kind in Sweden, have gone from idea to reality in just three months. Something that shows how large the interest is for co-creation.

- The most engaged people in this have never done anything similar before. When we started, a bunch of people started showing up that had just been waiting to get to create something. These people are everywhere, they just needed a place that allows for them to do what they want.

The participant Malin Bobeck testifies about the great openness that exists between the participants, and that because of this, it feels like everyone knows each other.

- This is a culture where we believe that people can do things. It is a very permissive environment, where one is allowed to try your ideas, alone or together with others, witch creates a great sense of belonging.

It doesn’t matter how much you contribute or with what.

- The construction is the most visible part, but everyone can contribute with their gift. Some contributes by giving hugs, for example. Gifting is an attitude, and not a measurement of how many nails you hammered in.

Gustaf Josefsson describes the festival like a large family gathering and said that it starts from the 10 principles of Burning Man, which stands as guidelines. Everyone takes a common responsibility.

- The fact that the event builds on shared values instead of rules brings out a more philosophical conversation about what we consider imporant.

The work to fill a giant air bubble that will display different video projections are going on fully and over the soft dance floor a giant radio controlled disco ball is spinning. Also visible, are the contours of a cosmic temple that will host a range of activities over the weekend.

- It is a temple for existential exploration, of yourself and of others, and will be an ongoing heartfelt experience, says Linus Lundkvist.

Beside a giant octopus stands Anna Lingren, sowing on one of the eyes. The octopus is the mascot for a similar forum in Malmö.

- We’re a large group of friends and new people are constantly joining. We’re going to have our own event this spring so it’s fun to see that these kinds of forums keep popping up.
