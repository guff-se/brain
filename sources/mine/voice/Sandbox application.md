- What makes you get up in the morning? *****

- The literal answer is that I actually suck at getting up in the morning. At night, the bug often grabs me, forcing me to work early into the morning without regard for sleep, food or any thoughts but the task ahead. I would say the ability to getting up in the morning is on my top list of personal improvements in progress. I've tried goal setting and focusing, I've tried set morning routines and automatic timer on the coffee machine. Still, my best trick is just to promise someone else that I will be in a certain location at a certain time. I might suck at getting up in the morning, but my drive to keep promises is stronger.

- If what you wanted to ask "what drives you", then this is a more appropriate answer:

- I like doing crazy shit.

- I have no interest in incremental innovation, I do not care about building just another good business, I want to fuck with peoples minds. I want to do the things that others consider to be impossible, irrational and even stupid. I want to challenge what others perceive to be "real". Be it starting a business where kids tell corporate ceos how to do their jobs, organizing spontaneous parties that brings together 3-4000 people in 72 hours, building the worlds largest piano, or doing inspiration talks that includes both one on one coaching, dance classes, acrobatics on stage, climbing on top of people's chairs and finishing with a mosh pit - i want to do things over the top.

- At the same time I have a long term goal: I want the knowledge, network and resources to be impulsive. I don't want to have to wait around for anyone else, I want to control my own time and minimize the distance from an idea in my head to market introduction. I want to build a system of people and organizations around me to be able to output more business / hour.

- List three of your biggest professional successes *****

- I would say my most important achievement is building the Entrepreneurial Chase (http://entreprenorsjakten.se, [http://entrepreneurialchase.com](http://entrepreneurialchase.com)) up to what it is today. We solve problems for corporations by letting young entrepreneurs compete in coming up with new solutions and then pairing the best solutions up with the best people. In a way we utilize parts of crowd sourcing, gamification and a networked business model to run a for-profit company with a social agenda of promoting young talents. Over the last three years we have worked with 20+ corporate clients, and 200+ young entrepreneurs as consultants, all across the country. Our main source of income are corporate clients, but last thursday we also landed a new €100 000 contract with the Swedish government to work with young entrepreneurs and further expand our business. We have recently expanded to Sydney, Australia, where our first major event is scheduled for this winter. I originally came up with the idea as a student in Chicago, and the organization I started there in 2007 still exists today, now having expanded to 8 schools in the mid-west area.

- Also, as a personal achievement, the network of young, brilliant minds I've surrounded myself with through this project is a true inspiration and a great value to my future endeavors.

- Second, being elected chairman of the board of Young Entrepreneurs of Sweden in 2009/2010 meant a lot to me. Not so much because of the formal responsibility it brought, but rather because of the respect and admiration I have for the people who elected me. I was the first member elected chairman and was lucky to get to be a part of shaping an organization that today brings together 200 of Sweden's finest young entrepreneurs.

- Finally, I don't have a real limit between professional and private, but I would also like to mention that I am an internationally recognized artist, through my work with the Grand Flat Piano. In 2010, we built a 25m long key board in Gothenburg, Sweden and shipped it to the Burning Man festival in Nevada, USA. It was funded with art grants from both Burning Man and the Swedish government and brought together 25 people to build it, spending a total of 1 000 hours of volunteer work. Building brands, networks and intellectual capital is fun, but to be able to point at a 600 kg art piece, and say: "I did that", is very rewarding.

- List organizations and places that shaped you as a person and how you shaped them *****

- Young Entrepreneurs of Sweden

- In 2002 i went to the "Young Entrepreneurship Week" (nowadays called Emax), an event that brought together some of the best 18-19-year-old entrepreneurs of sweden. I won the competition there and was sent on a trip to meet young entrepreneurs in Uganda. What happened to me that week and what happened because of that shaped my life. I came back to coach the kids there for five years. It was there I got some of the ideas for the Entrepreneurial Chase. It was also there that I met Fredrik Hagenius and got to be a part of the beginning of Young Entrepreneurs of Sweden. As I already mentioned, I was elected chairman there in 2010 and got to set the foundation of the open "by entrepreneurs for entrepreneurs", member driven organization that it is today.

- Delta Tau Delta Fraternity.

- As I was a foreign exchange student, I came in to the organization as the oldest guy in a house of 40 young men, 22 years old and 5 years younger than some of my peers. Through those 2,5 years I spent in the house, I became both an executive in the house, but most of all a community leader. With my age and experience became a mentor for some of the young guys who found themselves lost, away from friends and family, in charge of their own life at college. 

- My last semester (2007), I was in charge of recruitment, doing social training with the entire chapter (mind that this was a tech school), wrote a recruitment guide that is still used today. We managed to recruit 23 new guys, doubling our size and setting the record as the largest recruited class in the chapters 104 year history.

- In that house, I found some of the best friends I've ever had, I started to understand the politics and dynamics of large groups and I learned to accept and appreciate different personalities, values and strange quirks of human beings.

- Tell us the story of your biggest failure *****

- Last winter, I tried to expand the Entrepreneurial Chase across Sweden using a franchise model. I managed to get 10 young entrepreneurs to sign up, pay the upfront part of the franchise fee, go through training and get started.

- Out of the 10 startups, 2 succeeded. The other 8 cost me money, broken relationships with friends, many sleepless nights and a good amount of tears.

- In retrospect, I made several mistakes. First, I forgot how fucking hard it is to start up from scratch. I forgot the hardship I went through and the amount work I put in to start up the first event in Gothenburg in 2009. I wouldn't want to go through that again myself, and I didn't quite make it clear to my managers what they were facing. I think my ability to inspire others made things seem easier than they were. Since we were all geographically spread out across the country, reality hit pretty hard when they came home.

- Second, I didn't lead by example. My visions and dreams had moved on from the company that I had been running for the last 2-3 years. I was on my way out of the business and I made that too clear. In a situation like that, when you're suppose to lead and inspire people through a pretty tough sales process, you need to be all in or gtfo. I did neither.

- Third, I brought in too many people for too little work. Because running a local competition isn't full time work, I was unable to get full time dedication from my managers. They did other things (work, school, other businesses) at the same time and when you only have 50% of peoples attention, you need to make absolutely sure it is the first 50%. I didn't.

- Finally, I got the wrong people. Some were right, others weren't and I knew it. It all starts and ends with people and I should have trusted my guts.

- If you could talk to three persons in the past, who and why? *****

- I would like to talk to my dad when he was my age. We are so much alike in many ways and he did things in his youth that I really admire. Today, although we are still very close friends, his mind is not as open and curious as I know it must have been back in the day. His stories are amazing and he is a great inspiration to me, but he doesn't listen, he doesn't challenge him self and he doesn't change. I would love to get to know the young him.

- A more historical character I would have wanted to meet was Benjamin Franklin. I have a great admiration for the "polymath", jack-of-all-trades character that he represents. Seriously, not only was he a successful author, a renowned scientist, and a business man - he founded a frickin country!

- I don't se an entrepreneur as just a "person who starts a business". In my life I hope to start political parties, push technology, create magnificent art and maybe even found my own country.

- Finally, rather than meeting a person of the past, I would like to meet someone from the future. I have much respect for people who like history, but seriously, the future is pretty awesome. I don't care if you are JFK, Julius Caesar or Albert Einstein - i rather meet a 13-year-old kid from 2050.
