- What was the best part about being part of CORE for the 2012 Burning Man event? 

We did it! We can't believe we did it!

From a lofty idea of setting a ship on fire in a place far away, to a night that was one of the most rewarding experiences in our lives. But the road there was the true reward. Mostly because of the people we met along the way, all those amazing passionate, generous souls who made the project possible and the work both easy and fun.

- What was the biggest benefit? Was there any detriments? If so, what and why?

A part from the personal journey, the greatest benefit was that to our community. The viking ship project became the catalyst that brought the Swedish and Danish community together, both for the 100 people strong regional burn and the 80 people theme camp, Palace of Balunsia, at Burning Man.

- What was your biggest challenge?

On Monday, the week before the burn we get an email from U-Haul, saying they cancelled our reservation for our truck the next morning. The plan had been to get the truck on tuesday, pack it up, do some shopping and head out on early wednesday morning. Suddenly we have about 24 hours to solve our transport, with a nonexistent budget.

We sent out an email to, amongst others, the CORE list. One helpful soul from the Reno CORE project forwarded this email to the Reno regional email list and a couple of hours later we get a text message from Lacey Hosley, a Reno burner who tells us that her dad, Jerry, has a trailer that we might be able to use. Jerry, a retired construction worker and truck driver, have never been to Burning Man before and Lacey have for years tried to convince him to go. He offers us to take our ship out to BRC, spending a full day to drive there back and fourth, so we hook Jerry up with a ticket so that he can get through the gate.

As we are loading up the trailer, Jerry says that he's got "an offer we can't refuse", and offers us to keep the 20" trailer for the full week, and to come pick it up after the event. We happily accepted this offer, and throughout the day managed to convince him to come in on Saturday night to see the Man and the Temple burn.

After helping us getting all of our theme camp in to storage in Reno and offering us to stay in his house after the burn, Jerry is now committed to coming home next year and wants to be a part of our future projects. The Palace of Balunsia and the Swedish/Danish burner community have gotten our own dad in Reno. This complete strangers commitment and compassion meant the world to us.

- What changes would you suggest we make to our program?

We live in Sweden and had a problem with many of the times that were suggested for common online meetings. We are 9 hours ahead of you guys, so for next year it would be great if you gave the opportunity to schedule meetings in the morning (your time), so that we don't have to be up at 3 am on a weekday to attend them.

Also, we question the purpose of the mandatory general meetings. If you intend to distribute information to us, it is better to do it in written form, or any other way that we can take part of in our own time and that we can easily go back to. If you intend to truly engage in a conversation with us, then it's better to keep that in the one-to-one meetings. Letting us know that we should keep the speed limit on the high way to BRC is not something that has to be done through a big teleconference, but is better put in a bullet point list of things we need to think about. We also had severe problems with the quality of the teleconference, and since we're so many people in it, we couldn't ask for everything to be repeated.

Another thing that would be great is if you let next year's CORE projects know to never book trucks at U-Haul in Reno. They have a policy against letting their vehicles out in the desert, and they _will_ cancel when they find out (i.e. realize the dates of the booking). To have known this in advance would have spared us some sweat, but bereaved us of getting to know our savior Jerry… So, actually, don't tell people about it.

- Any burning thoughts you would like to share?

As we were doing our own fire safety meeting on the morning of the burn, we encouraged everyone in our camp to come help us out as perimeter crew. 19 people signed up and as we were setting up, most of them had showed up. As the time got closer, more and more people kept dropping in and asked to help out. We told them to keep filling up the perimeter and by the time we lit the fire, over 40 people were in the circle around the ship. We started singing our Swedish Viking Hymn as more and more people joined in. The people in the perimeter would allow the people around them to stand as a part of the circle, as long as they helped out to make sure no one got in. At one point, someone started to join hands, and as the ship was fully on fire, around 150 people were standing in a circle around the ship, holding hands and singing. It was a very personal, and highly emotional moment of unity that we hadn't expected.

We got this email last week:

"To the Naglfar crew,

Thank you for your wonderful CORE project at Burning Man. When I walked by on Thursday night, Naglfar was burning inside a ring of people dressed in Viking garb. The group was singing in a language I didn't know, but the sound and the sight of the ship burning brought tears to my eyes. The performance was the most beautiful thing I saw on the playa this year.

Thank you,

- scout"
