How can we make the deepest of life questions to be empowering instead of petrifying?

I want to give an inspirational talk on the importance of philosophy in a secular world. 

As I go to high schools and colleges I find so many kids hungering to talk about the big "why?"'s. Sweden is considered the "most atheist" country in the world. But with religion gone, who addresses these issues now? We are missing a new context to discuss those issues.

and I use these philosophical questions to challenge preconceived ideas and create motivation.

We all wrestle with questions like "who am I?", "what does it mean to be human?" and "what is the purpose of life?". Being able to cope with these are becoming increasingly more important, as the evolution of our species spins off into the 21st century.

In my talk, i combine these fundamental life questions with scientific fact and powerful music. It is half inspirational talk, half spoken word poetry, aimed at turning these questions into empowering curiousness.

I think we need to make philosophy a more central part in our culture and our everyday life and start talking openly about our fears and worries. Life is an endless path of possibilities and decision. Therein lies the horror and therein lies the beauty.

"For small creatures such as we, the vastness is bearable only through love." - Carl Sagan

I am a part of the "generation why?", born on the top of Maslow's pyramid, with nothing but a life self-actualization in front of me. I started questioning society, reality and the meaning of life as a 7-year-old. Since then, i've helped found several organizations that work with empowering young people and encourage them follow their dreams.

I've done many talks about entrepreneurship, dreams and the search for meaning. Over the last year, I have started working with music as a part of my talks. I put background music on power point slides and trigger these during parts of my lecture, creating a Hollywood-esque dramatic touch.

So, am I the BEST person to speak about "the meaning of life"?

Well, as I've found, no one else is doing it - and that is a big problem!
