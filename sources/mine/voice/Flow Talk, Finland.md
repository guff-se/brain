Arthur C. Clarke’s third law: “any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic.”

Hi,

Computers taught me to speak this language, the internet gave me these ideas. I am in this room today because of people I would not have known without Facebook. What is happening right now, my experience of being here on stage, and your experience of hearing me is created and facilitated by technology.

I am unimportant. I’m an output, a speaker connected to a larger network. To claim credit for the thoughts that I share with you today is hubris. I am merely an an expression of complexity beyond our understanding. I am co-created.

This is me, and I’m sitting in front of my Mac SE. Maybe someone in here had one?

The bus speed of this “computer”, the bus speed is basically the speed of the wire that goes between the memory and the processor in the computer. The speed of the wire between the two most important parts of the computer. The bus speed of this computer was the same speed as today’s 4G network.

So, the two most important parts inside this computer talked to each other at the same speed as my phone is peaking to Google’s servers while it’s in my pocket.

And still we call this “a computer”, while we often fail to realize that this is just a component. A component in the fastest computer we’ve ever built, the most accessible computer, reaching far across the world, the most used computer, that has still never crashed since it was turned on.

The Internet.

What is this? I mean, just look at the complexity. It’s to me fairly clear that this was obviously not “designed” by any single creator. There is just way too much stuff going on in here.

It goes back to the 70’s, the idea of the open architecture network, where anyone could connect whatever computer or other network they wanted, without central oversight. Anyone? Connecting anything? Anywhere? Without central oversight? It’s a pretty radical, anarchistic idea of co-creation.

What about CERN, in 1993, announcing that their new software, would be given for free to anyone, with no fees due. For anyone to read, add or edit, without central oversight. They called it the World Wide Web.

It’s a system that builds on individual initiative. Where permission isn’t needed. Where each node, each cell is allowed to create, to modify, to provide feedback and readjust. This is truly, the greatest example of co-creation in our history.

An emergent phenomena, where the whole is something fundamentally more than just the sum of the parts.

And it grew, it grew fast, powered by the exponentials of Moore’s law, that dictates that computing power doubles every 18 moths. Faster, faster, doubling, doubling. A rate of change that we humans cannot possible keep up with.

And that’s scary, right?

It’s the AI! It’s going to take our jobs! Half of our jobs within 20 years, according to a study at MIT, and pretty much no matter what futurologist you talk to nowadays, the message is the same. Be it 15 or 50 years still to go, the destiny of the general public is to no longer work for money. Quite simply because there is nothing that they (or we) can do that anyone else is willing to pay money for.

Steven Hawking and Elon Musk writes a paper to warn about the AI! Will it kill us? Will it take control of the world? Will it murder the innocent.

I’d like to just skip this talk bout the AI taking control over us. It already has control. It decides which music you listen to, which news you consume, it controls the stock market and use drones to assassinate people in Pakistan.

But to really understand the depth of what it means, I’d like to take a look at the expression itself. Because it describes our world.

Artificial Intelligence, what does it mean?

The word Artificial comes from the latin artificium, which means handicraft.

An artificial intelligence is a crafted intelligence. It is system, using based on a set of rules, that was created by someone. In the word we have already defined it as something that we will never accept as intelligent. As long as we understand how we created it, 

The intelligence of the human mind was never “created”, it emerged. An emergent intelligence, as opposed to a artificial, created intelligence is the result of complexity, not design, structure or intent. 

Let me give you an example of an artificial intelligence.

I read a post on Facebook earlier this week, from a 28 year old girl who had gotten a cancer relapse. After having gotten the information, she went straight into the hospital for over three weeks. As she got out and started her 3 months chemotherapy, she sent in the paperwork to the government to ask for her sick pay. She got the message back that she had failed to notify them within 7 days of getting sick, and hence could not get her money.

Does that seem intelligent to you? Any sensible person should be able to realize that not being able to fill out a form because you are sick should be a good enough reason in itself to qualify as sick.

Somewhere, in a maze of rules and bureaucracy, intelligence got lost.

Artificial intelligence is nothing more than our hubris, thinking that our limited minds can take this whole thing in and use a limited ruleset to describe it all. 

And this is how we keep building our world today. Using the model of the Napoleonic army.

Napoleon had good timing. Literacy had started sweeping the nation, which enabled him to this command-and-control structure, with clear lines of communication, like a complex machine, able to process vast amounts of information.

Because that is exactly what hierarchy is. It’s a computer.

It enables each person at the bottom to gather information and decide if they should report act on it immediately or report it upwards. The next person can then make the decision if this is something that concerns just us, or something that should keep traveling upwards. Data goes up, actions goes down and this structure is able to process all this data and act accordingly.

And this became the model of the industrial revolution. This is how we built or companies, and over the last centuries we’ve had this artificial intelligence of job descriptions and middle management bureaucrats span the world.

Organizations, especially the larger ones: multinationals and governments, still operate in this way

It’s an awesome machine.

But what if we can do more than just passing letters around? What if this person over here, using technology can find the exact information she is looking for, from this person over here, without all the people in between. Do we really need them anymore?
