Hi folks! I want to bring an issue to your attention ahead of the AGM tomorrow, concerning the Board, and the persistent issue of sexual assault at Burns.  
  
Since 2018, I and a group of others have flagged up significant concerns about a man, "X", who has a 20-year long record of domestic violence, sexual abuse and rape allegations against women in other countries. I am aware of at least 15 complaints from one sex-positive community and over 35 complaints in the next one. This man went through a community accountability process in Community 1, which produced no change, and went to abuse further in Community 2, where he went through a second lengthy accountability process, which again produced no change. He left that country in a hurry to avoid prosecution and is now here at Borderland on his third country, third community and third legal name. He has been de-certified, dropped by all previous workshop organisers and prevented from holding any events, by all of his previous contacts worldwide *except* one city in Scandinavia where he has joined the Borderland community and is now re-establishing activities.  
  
I should make clear that X has never apologised to the women affected or offered to put anything right. He has written (then swiftly removed due to legal liability) two face-saving admissions, then ran away when faced with real consequences.  
  
X has also written widely about his significant mental health diagnosis, which means he has unpredictable and uncontrolled violent outbursts when triggered (almost always by women). I have some professional training and experience in this specific area, and understand that these symptoms do not just go away. Treatment generally requires both long-term (10 years) intensive professional intervention, coupled with a personal willingness to change and to take responsibility for their actions. Neither are currently in place.  
  
After raising concerns about X being allowed to re-enter yet another sex-positive community, I and the group of others were met with a series of responses from Board members. I have attached one below so you can see an example of what we were met with. All replies were from white men and none of this was done on consultation with the wider community. I can sum up the response as follows:   
  
(i) "We follow radical inclusion, so we are letting him in"  
  
(ii) "He has changed! So we are not going to do anything"  
  
(iii) "We are keeping an eye on him and can handle this"  
  
(iv) "We should keep him in community where we can watch him, if we exclude him then he might go somewhere else"  
  
(v) not written but clear from the actions taken - "we are going to keep this private to ourselves and not make women at Borderland aware (e.g. women in his camp or women attending the workshops he holds)"  
  
(vi) "Where is your evidence?"  
  
I am now going to highlight some reasons why we feel these are all really fucking terrible ways to respond, and some suggestions for the bare minimum needed here to restore boundaries.  
  
(i) Radical inclusion. The 10 Principles are not rigid laws. Principles that might be sustainable during a week-long event are not always sustainable within a community, and indeed we already have to interpret other principles differently in practice. For example, "No Commerce" is nonsensical, as we have to buy our camp gear, costumes, food, and travel tickets in order to attend. We also let some Board members and others make money off their involvement in BL, whether through external consulting or via workshops/events advertised to BL community members who pay to attend. When it comes to radical inclusion, we need to interpret this too, by distinguishing between the person and their actions. An event without any boundaries whatsoever on behaviours is profoundly unsafe and also illegal. A man who has routinely battered and assaulted women without any personal apology or accountability needs to receive compassion as a human, but face suspension or exclusion on the basis of their behaviours, while those behaviours are being addressed externally. A mechanism needs to be in place to permit this, otherwise BL is inherently much less safe than 'default world' in ways that are invisible to, say, a new young female attendee. This is wrong and needs to change.  
  
Furthermore, by radically including X, you are simultaneously radically excluding a long list of X's female victims, for obvious reasons of physical safety. None of us are a match for X's physical strength and none of us want to meet him unexpectedly in a dark area at night given his record of violent uncontrolled reactions to us in the past (we already know we are triggering). Why should the Board prioritise X over the women affected, especially without any public consultation? Exclusion is already in play no matter what you do.  
  
(ii) For psychiatric reasons X is unable to change without long term skilled professional intervention. X's diagnosis means he is unable to understand what an appropriate boundary is, without significant external structuring from others. He experiences impaired insight into his own condition and the situations he finds himself in. He will tell you that black is white while believing it temporarily himself. X can sense people's emotions, but can't see a situation from another person's point of view, so he doesn't moderate his actions accordingly. Treatment needs to be performed by a neutral external professional NOT by a friend, housemate or community member, because the latter lack sufficient understanding or experience of psychiatric diagnoses and also because the blurred situation lacks boundaries, which is what X needs assistance with. X needs a referral, not hugs or more amateur workshops.  
  
(iii) Male board members can't be there 24/7 to "keep an eye on him". X is triggered by women and nearly all assaults have happened in front of women only. X knows how to manipulate and will ensure fawning behaviour around male power figures in the community. This will then flip like a light switch when the power tables are turned and he is alone with a vulnerable woman (I know this from my own experience).  
  
(iv) Two previous communities have attempted to handle this internally for years and have failed. While they were busy failing, more women got punched, assaulted and raped. The only action that changed X's behaviour was boundary setting via community exclusion and the threat of police involvement. I have seen and experienced for myself what X is like when faced with consequences and do not want to see any more of that violent rage vented on any of us. Do what works: refer X onwards for external treatment, exclude X from BL events and workshops and treat BL women with respect, instead of throwing our safety under a bus while suppressing the information, action and change needed to restore sanity. We need a process for suspending or excluding people like X and opening them to the 'defalt world' consequences of their actions, until people like X have nowhere to go but treatment (which is actually what they need). Anything else proactively enables abuse, while also prolonging the difficulties X continues to face in his life.  
  
(v) Issues like this, which affect the safety of unaware BL attendees, need to be handled with open consultation. This community promotes high-trust behaviours, so it needs to follow this up with transparency about how untrustworthy people will be handled and also with the boundaries needed to make BL at least as safe as 'default world'.   
  
We need to take on board here that X is not an isolated case. Statistically, as BL grows, we will inevitably end up with people like this. X's symptoms are shared with around 3% of men, so if it wasn't X, it would be someone else. This problem won't just go away if we ignore it, and in fact if we have poor policies and boundaries in this area then we can expect to attract much more than our fair share of people with these behaviours, because they will accumulate at BL after being excluded or made unable to operate elsewhere.  
  
(vi) I have shared my own experience with domestic violence with X while he was staying at my house for free (before I knew any of the above information). I have also witnessed a number of other women's experiences with X and have shared non-identifying accounts of those situations. I have also offered to put Board members in touch with the two previous community organisers. So far, no response, and to my knowledge they have not been contacted. Beyond that I am not able to pass on personal accounts from other women, because those were shared in private. That is a matter for the police and frankly that is where this needs to be referred to. Board members are completely unqualified and also in the wrong position to act as judge, jury, psychiatrists or policemen, and they should not place themselves in this role. Instead we need clear policies placing the "authority" of the Board under limits, specifying when to suspend or exclude a person, which authorities to contact and which support services to refer persons like this on to.  
  
** Legal liability: In the UK, where I am from, the Board of organisations like BL would have a legal duty of care towards BL members. Admitting a person like X, after hearing this level of evidence and complaints but not acting on them, would open up the Board to legal action, especially if any more women got assaulted or raped at BL. The issue would be failure to take adequate measures to protect BL members and/or failure to put in place appropriate policies to do so. This needs attention. **  
  
I personally feel that a lot of care needs to be taken with the Teal organisational experiment currently going on in the BL community. BL is not really well defined enough to function as an organisation (who is even in the "community"?) and we lack the behavioural and performance standards plus barriers to entry that any 'default world' organisation would have.  To be Teal in any way though, you need Teal people, and there currently isn't much to deter the aggressive self-interested 'Red' ones who also show up. Teal is a nice ideal, but more policies and boundaries are needed to prevent Red people from volunteering their way into positions of power which they will promptly use for abuse and/or financial gain. Some people see BL as a big smorgåsbord of paying clients or attractive sexually available hippy women, and those people will show up interested in taking what they can get.  
  
Here are some recommendations to handle X and people like him better.  
  
(1) Make available a clear written description of the role and duties of the Board members, and ensure that the "authority" of Board members is limited to those minimum duties *unless* there is a public consultation otherwise. Put consequences in place for Board members who overstep their role, e.g. removal from the Board.  
  
(2) Write a list of specific actions that will get you suspended or excluded from Borderland and post this alongside the 10 Principles wherever they appear. We may need to specify in particular which illegal or abusive activities will not be tolerated here.  
  
(3) Write a list of external persons in police departments, hospitals, and support services who can be contacted in the case of receiving allegations of abuse or problematic illegal behaviours.  
  
(4) Form a clear written policy on what to do in the case of unacceptable behaviours being reported, and state who will do it. For example, nominate a contact person in each country whose role is to hear allegations, initiate internal action, and pass the person on to the relevant external contacts, depending on where it happened, *without* requiring the affected person to disclose more identifying details than they feel safe doing. This might be a member of a consent team. Given the huge gender split in who rapes and assaults whom, I suggest the contact persons should not be men. Part of the point should be to get around the problem of women staying silent because their abuser has manipulated his way into a position of social power and now has friends on the team.  
  
(5) Obtain legal advice regarding the resulting policies and procedures, and on any liability of the Board relating to their actions in a case like this. Adapt accordingly.  
  
Whatever you decide, it needs to be fit to address this current situation.  
  
There is plenty more that I could say and discuss here, but I feel it's important to stick to the main points. I have my own boundaries around this issue, these include that I'm not available for abusive comments or messages, nor am I available for mediation with X, or a "let's solve this with a chat", because what's required is a specific change in policy and actions. There is more currently under way from the side of people affected, so you can expect that this won't just go away. That's what I am choosing to do on behalf of the safety and integrity of myself and other women. Now the rest is over to you.
