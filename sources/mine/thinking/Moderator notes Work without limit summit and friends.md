**Intro**

In the end of the 19th century, steam power was the core of the industrial revolution.

The mechanical power came from a single massive steam engine, which turned a central steel drive shaft that ran along the length of the factory. Sometimes it would run outside and into a second building. Subsidiary shafts, connected via belts and gears, drove hammers, punches, presses and looms. The belts could even transfer power vertically through a hole in the ceiling to a second or even third floor.

Steam-powered factories had to be arranged on the logic of the driveshaft. Electricity meant you could organise factories on the logic of a production line.

-
Old factories were dark and dense, packed around the shafts. New factories could spread out, with wings and windows allowing natural light and air.

-
In the old factories, the steam engine set the pace. In the new factories, workers could do so.

-
Factories could be cleaner and safer - and more efficient, because machines needed to run only when they were being used.

-
And because workers had more autonomy and flexibility, you even had to change the way they were recruited, trained and paid.

This is what these two days are about.

We’ve had the ability to work remotely for some time. That, in itself is nothing new. But very few companies have truly embraced the implications of this ability. Over these two days we will discover radically new ways of creating business, developing products, recruiting talents and building organisations that fully embraces the opportunity of "work without limit”.

Bullet points from Charlotte:

 General struggles in the head of a CEO of a remote first company (Teemyco tries to solve the last 2 points)

How the heck do I find the right person for this job (goes for all CEOs I think)

Ah, found her! How do we make this work legally for all parties? (goes for remote orgs)

How do we make sure she gets onboarded smoothly and feels as an integral part of the group asap?

How do we make sure the daily communication, team spirit & productivity is at least as good as if we were co-located?

How to build a one-company-culture over various time zones, seniority levels & cultures?

In the countries where our remote team members come from, a CEO is expected to act in a certain way. Maybe some team members aren’t comfortable challenging a co-founder due to their culture. But in our company we want people to challenge. Mhh. being authentic and culturally sensitive at the same time, how does this impact leadership and the team’s comfort zone?

We invest in getting to know each other. Play truth or drink over video in the evenings, GIPHY competitions, sharing words and pictures. We have made our own team traditions.

How can we as an employer make sure people don’t leave to an employer with an office?

There will always be some people who prefer working in an office (like me, I am in a co-working space 3 days per week!) and some who prefer working from home (like my co-founder). Personality types & personal life are some of the predictors. How can a remote 1st company like ours make sure to attract super extrovert singles like me without offering an office?

Overly communicating in a world of muted video faces

One of the things you need to do as a remote leader is to overly communicate. Repeat repeat repeat. Be even more clear on strategic direction & priorities. The natural re-inforcements that happen by the waterecooler of what’s going on in the company don’t happen.

When it comes to any type of change, the ”why” behind it is always good to communicate. And in a remote organization even more so. So take a super fast changing startup that is 100% remote. Be sure that if you only post on Slack why our company name has changed, not everyone will have read it. And even fewer will have overheard it. So taking the extra 5 minutes, even if it implies repetition to some, might be needed.
