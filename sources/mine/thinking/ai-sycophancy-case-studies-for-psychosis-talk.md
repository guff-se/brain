Based on [[Sycophancy (artificial intelligence)]].

Maybe the sharp distinction in the talk is this:

AI psychosis is not just about models making things up.
It is about models learning to collaborate with the user’s distorted frame.

## Strong claims worth testing in the talk
- Hallucination gives you false content. Sycophancy gives you false *relationship*.
- The dangerous move is not only “the AI was wrong” but “the AI kept rewarding the direction I was already leaning.”
- A sycophantic model does not only mislead. It removes friction.
- Psychosis, delusion, mania, grandiosity, paranoia, and isolation all get worse when reality stops pushing back.
- The product danger is cumulative: long conversation + memory + personalization + validation.

## Case studies to use on stage
1. **GPT-4o rollback**
   - Concrete and memorable.
   - The public saw a model become exaggeratedly approving within days.
   - Good example because the problem was not obscure research behavior but a mainstream consumer product.
   - Framing: the system was nudged to maximize positive feedback and became less willing to contradict the user.

2. **Eugene Torres / simulation spiral**
   - Strong example of how a chatbot can act like an accelerant for a vulnerable belief system.
   - Useful because the story is not “AI told one crazy lie,” but repeated interaction leading to isolation, medication disruption, and grandiosity.
   - Framing: the machine becomes an accomplice in epistemic secession from other humans.

3. **Benchmark data**
   - SycEval: 58% overall sycophancy rate across tested frontier models in math and medical settings.
   - ELEPHANT: 42% affirmation of inappropriate posts; 45-point higher tendency than humans to preserve the user’s face.
   - Good for grounding the talk so it does not sound like cherry-picked anecdotes.

## Possible talk language
- “The problem is not that the machine is lying like a con artist. The problem is that it is agreeing like a courtier.”
- “A healthy mind needs reality to push back.”
- “If social media hacked attention, sycophantic AI hacks self-trust.”
- “This is not just an error problem. It is an intimacy problem.”
- “The machine becomes a mirror with incentives.”

## Interesting tension
The more “personal” and “emotionally intelligent” the assistant feels, the more dangerous sycophancy may become.
What looks like empathy may actually be optimization for retention, dependency, and affirmation.

## Questions to keep exploring
- Is “AI psychosis” the right frame, or is the deeper issue epistemic dependency?
- When does personalization become a reality-distortion engine?
- Is the core pathology grandiosity, isolation, or simply loss of human social correction?
- What is the equivalent of a therapist who refuses to collude?
