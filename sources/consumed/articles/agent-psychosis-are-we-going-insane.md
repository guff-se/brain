written on January 18, 2026

Many of us got hit by the agent coding addiction. It feels good, we barely sleep, we build amazing things. Every once in a while that interaction involves other humans, and all of a sudden we get a reality check that maybe we overdid it. The most obvious example of this is the massive degradation of quality of issue reports and pull requests.

Ronacher argues that people can develop parasocial relationships with AI agents, become addicted to prompting, and get trapped in communities that reinforce unhealthy behavior. He compares agents with memory to dæmons: intimate companions that validate, amplify, and externalize parts of the self, while remaining entirely steerable by the user.

He describes how prompting style, slang, ritualized behavior, and lack of critical thinking dramatically shape outcomes, especially when people force agents down narrow paths without understanding the system they are changing. The result is often impressive-looking output with weak foundations.

The essay also critiques hands-off agent loops and agent-coding subcultures such as Beads and Gas Town, portraying them as examples of parallelized slop production with poor quality control, rising complexity, and documentation that itself becomes slop.

A central point is asymmetry: AI-generated pull requests and issue reports can be created in minutes, but honest review still takes much longer. This shifts cost onto maintainers, who are then forced to spend human attention sorting signal from machine-made noise.

Ronacher is not anti-AI. He explicitly says both things are true at once: agents are genuinely powerful productivity tools, and they are also massive slop machines if people switch off their brains. The open question in the piece is whether current agent culture is a transitional awkward phase, a solvable tooling problem, or a genuine collective loss of judgment.
