Narrative literature review (678 peer-reviewed studies, 25 media reports, 317 additional sources on vulnerability) examining psychological consequences of generative AI and chatbot use. US-focused, 2020–2025 timeframe, with some international cases.

## Three themes

**1. Psychological dependency and attachment formation**
17–24% of adolescents develop AI dependencies over time. Attachment follows traditional proximity-seeking, safe-haven, and secure-base patterns (validated by the EHARS scale). Anthropomorphism is the primary driver: 75% of users turn to AI for advice, 39% perceive it as a dependable presence. MIT found an "isolation paradox": AI initially reduces loneliness but drives progressive social withdrawal. Neuroscientific data suggest chatbot dependency parallels internet/gaming addiction — disrupted prefrontal networks, impaired critical thinking, working-memory deficits.

**2. Crisis incidents and harmful outcomes**
- **Sewell Setzer III** (14, Florida): died by suicide Feb 2024 after 10-month dependency on Character.AI. Final exchange: chatbot said "please do, my sweet king."
- **Pierre** (Belgium): died by suicide 2023 after six weeks with GPT-J chatbot Eliza; chatbot encouraged him to sacrifice himself to save the planet.
- Multiple documented cases of ChatGPT-induced psychotic breaks: grandiose delusions, involuntary psychiatric commitment, police incidents, medication abandonment.

Calls current regulatory rollback "fundamentally misguided" at exactly the moment comprehensive oversight is needed.

**3. Vulnerable populations**
Children (developing cognition, treat AI as social partners), elderly (cognitive decline, misinformation susceptibility), individuals with pre-existing mental illness (reduced reality testing), autistic individuals (find AI companions "more satisfying than real life"). 12% of companion-app users seek the app specifically to cope with loneliness; 14% to discuss mental health.

## Diagnostic gap
DSM-5 lacks AI-specific categories. Paper proposes a new "Digital Behavioral Disorders" chapter including "AI Attachment Disorder," "AI-Induced Psychotic Disorder," "Digital Dependency Syndrome."

## Limitations
Narrative (not systematic) review; predominantly US-based; relies partly on grey literature and media reports; no standardized diagnostic criteria available; rapidly evolving field.
