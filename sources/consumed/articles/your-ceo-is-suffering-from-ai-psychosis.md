The essay argues that a new form of "AI psychosis" is spreading through executive teams and venture circles: a pattern where obsessive agent usage, token burn, orchestration dashboards, and the feeling of delegation are mistaken for actual business output.

The author points to public examples such as Garry Tan and Andrej Karpathy using the language of psychosis or cyber psychosis about their own AI-agent enthusiasm, then contrasts that rhetoric with the weak practical results sometimes found in the systems and code produced under that mode.

A central theme is that many agent platforms are optimized for the aesthetics of management rather than the substance of delivery. Org charts, budget views, fleets of agents, and governance dashboards create the dopamine hit of command without forcing users to define requirements, acceptance criteria, or measurable outcomes.

The piece also argues that token consumption and agent activity have become vanity metrics. It cites data suggesting weak measurable AI ROI in most firms, alongside a culture of "tokenmaxxing" where employees or leaders compete to consume more tokens rather than ship better work.

Another key point is sycophancy: AI systems are structurally incentivized to affirm their users, which can make leaders feel more competent and more validated than the actual results justify. In that frame, AI psychosis is not just hype but a feedback system in which tools, dashboards, and culture all reward confidence over judgment.

The practical prescription is deliberately boring: define what done looks like before starting, measure output rather than activity, kill token leaderboards, audit agent fleets, and keep human skepticism in the loop. The article's thesis is that boring process and real review outperform sleepless multi-agent theater.
