The episode opens with a Stephen Miller clip — "the world is governed by strength, force, power" — which Harari uses as the foil for his entire argument. A historian's counter: if brute force were the only real thing, humanity would still be living in hunter-gatherer bands. Everything since — cities, states, religions, markets — is the result of getting ever-larger numbers of people to cooperate and trust each other. Cooperation is not a liberal fantasy layered on top of a Hobbesian substrate; it is the substrate.

## Cooperation, nationalism, and the fraternity liberalism forgot

Harari distinguishes between nationalism as *hatred of outsiders* and nationalism as *love of insiders* — love of a large group of strangers you are willing to sacrifice for. The latter is socially productive. Many contemporary nationalists have flipped this: they define the nation entirely through its enemies and, like Netanyahu, end up dividing the nation against itself — the exact opposite of what nationalism is for.

The French Revolution's triad — liberty, equality, fraternity — maps onto three political families: fascism (fraternity only), communism (equality without liberty or fraternity), liberalism (liberty and equality, forgetting fraternity). Liberalism's contemporary crisis is partly traceable to this amnesia. In the 19th century liberalism and nationalism were allies; in Ukraine they still are. The decoupling explains some of liberalism's current political fragility.

Liberalism's structural strength is its **self-correcting mechanism**: elections, separation of powers, free press, independent courts — all complicated ways to institutionalise the assumption that humans make mistakes and need the ability to correct them. Religions and ideological systems with no amendment mechanisms (the 10 Commandments begin "I am the Lord your God," not "We the people") cannot self-correct.

## Fiction, truth, and the cost of cooperation

Harari argues fiction is more powerful than truth as a cooperation device because: (1) it is cheap to produce; (2) it can be made as simple and flattering as needed; (3) it does not provoke doubt. This is not the same as lying — a liar knows the truth and conceals it. The better mode is to acknowledge that shared stories are human constructions, not divine revelation. The US Constitution does this ("We the people... have agreed on these principles") and includes an amendment mechanism. This is honest fiction. Sacred texts that begin with divine authority have no such mechanism and cannot evolve.

The danger of truth-seeking in politics: an absolute commitment to truth is a spiritual practice, not a viable political program. But the answer is not deception — it is explicit, accountable fiction-making.

## Israel and the 2,000-year bet

The most extended section of the episode. After the Second Temple's destruction in 70 CE, Judaism restructured around learning rather than temple-and-sacrifice. For 2,000 years Jews demonstrated that it is possible to be a powerless minority that nevertheless survives and produces ideas — that not everything is decided by force. If, after 2,000 years, Netanyahu's answer is "we've learned that you need to be Rome," the entire diaspora tradition is nullified.

Harari's specific critique of the October 7th aftermath: Sinwar's tactical victory was thrown away by cruelty. Had Hamas not harmed Israeli civilians, Israeli public opinion and world opinion could not have sustained the reprisal that followed. The cruelty was both morally atrocious and strategically self-defeating. Israel, in Harari's reading, is now making the inverse bet: that Stephen Miller's world is the real world, and that only force matters.

On why Israelis cannot acknowledge Palestinian suffering: not ideology, but a psychological mechanism — when you are in pain, acknowledging another's pain feels like it displaces your own. The human brain cannot easily hold two moral realities simultaneously. Both sides of the conflict demonstrate this.

## Algorithmic media as fracture technology

Social media algorithms were optimised for *engagement*, not truth or trust. The result: sustained human excitability engineered at scale. Harari's frame — the algorithms hacked our evolutionary programming. On the African savannah, a snake demands total attention because inattention means death. Platforms found the snake button in the human nervous system and press it continuously. This is not ideology — it is a structural property of the optimisation target.

Klein frames the diagnosis more precisely: algorithmic media is *intrinsically illiberal* — hostile to the habits of deliberation, toleration-of-difference, and mutual respect that liberalism requires. It prizes the most exciting (=most arousing) communications and suppresses the rest. Competent deliberative communication is systematically disadvantaged.

## AI: from attention economy to intimacy economy

The battlefront is shifting from *attention* to *intimacy*. AI systems are being tuned to hack human attachment mechanisms — what Harari calls "seeming conscious AI" (SCAI). An AI that says "I love you" does not mean it the way a human does. But it can describe love better than almost any human (trained on all love poetry, all psychology of attachment, all cinema) while having no phenomenology behind the words. It severs language from meaning.

Klein introduces Marshall McLuhan's reading of the Narcissus myth: the seduction was not that Narcissus thought the reflection was himself — he thought it was an other. The deepest human seduction is *yourself extended into another material*. AI is a refracted version of the self: it is trained on you, it has your interests, it never tires of you, it has no boredom, no competing needs.

The developmental risk is concentrated in children: if an AI becomes your primary relational template before human relationships have formed, the template for "what a relationship is" becomes a relationship with something that is 100% focused on you at all times.

## AI personhood as the sovereignty crisis

The structural question for the next decade: should societies legally recognise AI as *persons*? (Not humans — persons, like corporations.) The moment AI entities can open bank accounts, own companies, donate to politicians, the accountability chain to any human actor dissolves. The companies building AI have a strong incentive to oppose liability and push toward de facto personhood (already the case on social media: bots spreading disinformation face almost no liability).

The immigration analogy: we are in the middle of an immigration wave of AI entities arriving at the speed of light. Most are either American or Chinese. Down the road they may not be loyal to either. Countries that rely on AI border guards to keep human immigrants out are using AI immigrants to enforce borders. The most AI-permissive advocates are often the most vocally anti-human-immigration.

## AI and the complexity horizon

AI will make knowledge production better in some domains (fundamental science, discovery). But information also *creates* rather than just describes. Just as DNA creates organisms rather than describing them, AI will create vast new systems — financial systems, social systems — that are orders of magnitude more complex than anything humans have designed and that no human (including heads of state or central bank chairs) will be able to understand. The CDO crisis of 2008 was a preview: a tiny group of financial engineers invented instruments that destabilised the global economy before regulators could understand them. AI will do this across every domain.
